Abstract

Novel view synthesis from only a few input images is increasingly demanded across virtual and augmented reality, autonomous driving, and robotics, where a scene must be rendered convincingly from viewpoints that were never captured. In sparse-view, unbounded outdoor settings, however, the severe lack of information in unobserved regions makes novel view synthesis especially difficult: conventional methods produce distorted geometry and sharp quality degradation precisely in the regions a viewer is most likely to look. Pseudo-views generated by a video diffusion model can supply the missing synthesis cues for these regions, but they are not always reliable—containing hallucinated structures, temporal drift, inconsistent geometry, and pixel-level uncertainty—so using them directly as supervision degrades synthesis quality, while aggressively filtering them removes coverage that sparse-view synthesis cannot afford to lose.

To improve novel view synthesis under these conditions, this thesis proposes GradeS, a geometry-aware, reliability-guided diffusion supervision framework. Its key idea is to treat the per-point confidence produced by a dense stereo reconstruction model as a single unified reliability signal that propagates through the entire synthesis pipeline. Before generation, the scene-grounding renderer is stabilized via camera-proximity layer splitting, bootstrap depth regularization, and confidence-driven floater suppression, yielding clean conditioning. During generation, a confidence-modulated stage-wise latent blending preserves reliable observed regions while letting the diffusion model synthesize only the unobserved ones. After generation, the supervisory influence of each view is weighted by hallucination detection and frame- and pixel-level reliability, turning generated frames into reliability-controlled supervision for unseen-region synthesis. Experiments on unbounded outdoor scenes show that the method achieves more stable geometry and higher-quality novel view synthesis under limited input, and that the iterative reconstruction scheme outperforms applying Gaussian Splatting only once after a single generation step.

Method

Given sparse input views with known camera poses, we initialize a Gaussian field from a point cloud (e.g. DUSt3R) and render guidance trajectories with a frozen base renderer. A video diffusion model refines these trajectories into consistent pseudo-observations, which are distilled back into the Gaussian field during optimization.
